Projects

Project 1: Interactive Audio & Visual with p5.js

Final Sketch

This project is driven by my passion for raising awareness about Korea’s declining birthrate and aging population. I wanted to experiment with data visualization while incorporating audio to make the experience more engaging.

The first graph uses bar heights and baby-crying sounds to represent birth numbers, the second shows bouncing population circles whose sound volume changes with size, and the third visualizes the super-aged population with branching veins. Users can return home by pressing ESC.

Project 2: HyperNarrative

Kodokushi

This project explores the quiet loneliness behind kodokushi, or “lonely death,” a social issue in Japan where people pass away alone. Memories of seeing news about it as a child resurfaced as I learned how the issue continues to grow, making me reflect on isolation and fading social connection.

Slow pacing, muted colors, and minimal design mirror the stillness and emotional distance of the topic, encouraging viewers to pause and absorb the weight of overlooked quiet moments.

Project 3: Networked Care

Project 3

I created this website to share the story of Korea’s population crisis in a way that feels clear, accessible, and visually engaging. Drawing from my p5.js data visualization project, I reworked key elements into simple, interactive graphics.

The site focuses on three interconnected issues: Korea’s declining population, the rapid growth of its aging demographic, and the ongoing drop in birth rates. My hope is to raise global awareness, especially among non-Korean audiences.

Vibe Coding

Korean Food Recommender

I created a Korean Food Recommender website that suggests personalized Korean dishes based on user preferences like spice level, sweetness, weather, and recent meals. The site features a modern, culturally-inspired design with a clean form interface that leads to a results page showing the top 3 recommended dishes with scores and explanations.

Anti-Friendly Hair Collection Game

This is an interactive hair collection game where you have to click on scattered hair strands on a tiled floor to clean them up. I made it intentionally frustrating - even when you successfully collect all the hair, the system shows an "ERROR" message and forces you to start over again, inspired by the anti-user-friendly design concept introduced by Yehwan Song. The hair strands are scattered across a simple beige tile floor, and there are always 5-6 pieces to collect each round.

Loves Me, Loves Me Not

For this vibe-coding assignment, I created an interactive "loves me, loves me not" flower picking game featuring five randomly generated flowers with 7-13 petals each. Users select a flower and watch the petals fall one by one while text alternates between "he loves me" and "he loves me not," culminating in an overwhelming cascade of chaotic flying text. I drew inspiration from mayaontheinter.net's experimental Y2K aesthetics, featuring neon gradients and glitch effects.

Social Battery Receipts

For this vibe coding experiment, I took inspiration from Molly Soda’s artist page aesthetic to make a social battery tracker that feels playful and personal. Social interactions show up as receipt-style entries, with glittery borders, pixel textures, and soft pastel tones that echo her diary-like vibe. It turns into a kind of digital collage where each receipt is a small record of vulnerability, connection, and the “cost” of hanging out. In the end, it makes social exhaustion feel more intimate and poetic, but also a little fun.

Fragments of Memory

My vibe-coding experiment was inspired by Winnie Soon's Unerasable Characters III, exploring how childhood memories fragment, fade, and reconstruct over time. The page asks a series of intimate questions, collecting personal recollections and transforms them into a child-like diary entry where answered questions become narrative fragments and skipped questions manifest as visual glitches and blurred text. By treating memory as data that can be incomplete, corrupted, or lost, the page functions to reveal how absence and erasure are as meaningful as what we remember, creating a poetic visualization of the gaps between experience and recollection.

Daenggi

This vibe coding experiment is an interactive, poetic space inspired by Stephanie Dinkins’ idea of the unencumbered mind in Afro-nowism, reimagined through Korean cultural memory. I use flowing digital silk strands like daenggi ribbons to explore how remembrance and liberation intertwine, with each gesture of untying and retying revealing soft Korean and English whispers. It’s my way of creating a quiet, meditative Korean futurism that blends care, continuity, and digital breath.

Recognition Journey

I created a vibe coding experiment about recognition and honoring the visible and invisible connections that shape who we are. It asks reflective questions about your life— where you're from, who taught you, what brings you comfort—and generates personalized recognitions based on your answers. The piece surfaces relationships you might not have considered: the indigenous peoples whose land you inhabit, the hands that prepared your meals, the workers who assembled your devices. It's rooted in principles from "nonviolent creative code," exploring how we can create work that honors the land, the body, the small, and the people without extracting or exploiting.

Thought Drop

After reading Brandon Gregory's work on Designing for Cognitive Differences, I vibe-coded a memo website that removes the anxiety of organization and provides constant reassurance through clear feedback. The design focuses on inclusive principles like confirmation messages that tell you exactly what action was taken and when, generous spacing to reduce cognitive load, and calming language that validates the simple act of writing thoughts down. I used a minimal palette (black, white, light beige) and varied typography to create clear visual hierarchy while keeping the interface distraction-free so users can capture random thoughts without any pressure to categorize or perfect it.

Why Did You Cry?

I created "Why did you cry?" which is a digital sanctuary inspired by Xin Liu's tear series, where emotions become tangible through water droplets. Each tear recorded transforms into a droplet sized by its intensity, building a personal collection of emotional moments that honors the vulnerability of crying. The interface invites users to witness their accumulated tears as a beautiful, floating constellation of experiences, reframing sadness as something worth preserving and reflecting upon.

Live Code Studio

I created an interactive web studio that generates p5.js visual art variations based on user-customized parameters like shapes, colors, and animations, inspired by Olivia Jack's artist website. I wanted to make something practical while also testing AI's ability to generate visual outputs and the underlying code structure. The result is a playground where anyone can experiment with generative design, see the code behind their creations, and explore how simple parameters can produce endlessly looping variations.

Vibe Coding Reflection

This semester was my first time working with vibe coding, and it changed the way I think about building digital work. I was genuinely surprised by how quickly simple ideas developed into functioning websites. Seeing something move from a simple idea or thought to a working form in such a short amount of time made the process feel very approachable. At the same time, I became aware of the limitations. Some platforms had difficulty understanding my requests even when I rephrased them, and the results often drifted away from my original intention. In those moments, I was always reminded back to the thought that AI is powerful but not entirely reliable.

As I continued the exercises I began to notice a particular feeling in many vibe coded sites. Certain visual and verbal choices made them easy to distinguish from websites created through more traditional methods. The color decisions, the structure, and even the phrasing carried a recognizable tone that felt consistent across different projects.

However, I do see a lot of potential in vibe coding. I believe that it is useful for building a starting point and for testing ideas before committing to a fully developed design. I still believe the creator needs to guide and refine the work afterward in order to give it depth, intention, and a sense of life.
